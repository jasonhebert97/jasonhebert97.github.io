Office of Sustainability Marketing Plan

As Brown University's Office of Sustainability transitioned to using the general University's visual theming policies and standards, it was important to effectively articulate the Office's new marketing tactics and streamline the entire flow for the interns. This project involves restructuring the marketing and design flow of the Office. All materials presented below were made and organized by me.

Marketing Components

The newly developed marketing plan consists of multiple elements that all interconnect. Below are the different components that are used by all the interns in regards to all marketing pathways. The integral components consist of social media, today@brown, and graphics request and submissions handling; data visualization templates; and other visual templates.

Social Media, Today@Brown, & Graphics Requests

The new marketing plan allows interns to submit social media, Today@Brown (a proprietary newsletter sent to each community member daily), and graphics requests through one vantage point.

The interns have two options For submitting social media requests: one being a time-sensitive form (for those posts requiring more specific dates and times for posting) and the other a filler form (for those posts that are meant to fill in any space between time-sensitive posts). Today@Brown submissions and graphics requests are handled similarly to social media submissions but do not require multiple forms.

Each of the forms was made using Google Forms which automatically collects the data and pre-populates respective Google Sheet calendars that highlight the posting date or first draft due date. Examples of the forms and automated sheets are shown below.

The Sheets formula used is shown here: =IFERROR(VLOOKUP(F6,SubmissionDates,2,FALSE), "") where F6 is the date on the calendar in question and SubmissionDates represents the entire set of data on the collected submissions.

Data Visualization Templates

An important addition to the marketing plan was creating a streamlined means of producing data visualizations that were mindful of the newly accepted university visual standards. Four different types of graphs were made into templates: vertical bar, horizontal bar, line, and pie. All templates were made using Microsoft Excel and require minimal visual changes from the user.
