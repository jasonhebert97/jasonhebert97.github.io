Personas & Storyboard

The projectors in Sayles Hall at Brown University are supposed to do as their name implies: project some sort of image or video from a computer screen to a projector screen. There are two methods for doing this, one wired and the other wireless. The former method is relatively intuitive; however, the latter is far more complicated and lacks practical affordances. The goal for this project is to collect qualitative data and curate two personas presenting differing mental models as well as to create a single storyboard going through an imagined scenario of one of those personas. This project was completed as an assignment for the class User Interface / User Experience (CSCI1300) at Brown University to showcase familiarity with the methodologies for curating user-interface personas and storyboards.

Background

The interface consists of two separate connection methods. The first is labelled “Laptop” which requires the user to physically connect their device to the projector unit via an array of cords and dongles. This option is only useful for those with compatible devices and close-range connections however. To combat this, the second method — titled “AirMedia” — utilizes a wireless connection alleviating any physical constraints such as device incompatibility and distance. Research Methodology & Findings

To collect data, my partner and I reached out to 6 different people from varying communities to come in, answer some questions, and operate the machinery. We began with 4 pre-task interview questions to retrieve baseline information to aid us in developing mental models. Shortly afterwards, we had the interviewee attempt to project an image from a control computer used throughout all interviews using both the “Laptop” and “AirMedia” methods. Once completed, or once they have given up on the task, we asked 4 post-task questions to retrieve more information on their ideas of the interface after using it. Observations were made throughout the entirety of the session. All of the questions and findings asked are listed below:

  1. How many times have you personally operated a projector so far this school year?
  2. What do you personally use projectors for? You may select multiple answers:
    1. To give presentations
    2. To facilitate collaboration on projects
    3. To watch movies or play games
    4. Other
  3. What type(s) of media do you consume through projectors? Lectures? Presentations?
  4. How do you think this projector works? Can you discern any key elements or features?

  1. After trying both methods of projection, which would you say you prefer? Why?
      AirMedia way because…
      Laptop way because…
      No preference because…
  2. What things about the interface bothered you? Did you notice any “pain points”?
  3. (Again) How do you think the projector works? Can you discern any key elements or features?
  4. Do you have any further questions, concerns, or comments?

Following data collection, the information was condensed and generalized into two types of responses from two different types of personalities. The first personality, named Techie Tarry, encapsulates the responses of those that felt comfortable or familiar with the interface or similar technologies. The second, named Bookworm Barry, generalizes the responses of those that felt uncomfortable or unfamiliar with the technology. It should be noted that this dichotomy is extremely generalized; they are mainly used to denote the main differences found amongst the interviewees. The summarized responses for the two fabricated personalities are written below:

Techie Tarry

Techie Tarry has used projectors like this in the past, though not recently. They have a wealth of knowledge of modern technology and feels comfortable navigating new interfaces. They see the benefits of both wired and wireless media, and know where to look for troubleshooting help when needed.

Bookwarm Barry

Bookworm Barry has seen projectors used in different settings like classes and group meetings, but has almost never operated one themself. They do not keep up with modern technology much and is more comfortable using familiar machines.

Personas

Humanizing the two personalities, generalized above in the general findings section, into client personas will help in improving the system to account for different types of users. Below are fabricated personas that portray different mental models and empathy maps.

Techie Tarry

Mental Model

The projector works in two ways: the first uses wires and the second is wireless. The former involves creating a physical connection between the projector and laptop via the supplied cords; however, the latter utilizes a wireless connection generated by means of a supplied application.

Empathy Map

  • I wish this projector was more accessible.
  • Outlining the steps for AirMedia would help a lot.
  • The cords are useful, but only if the device has compatible ports.

  • Pressured to figure it out.
  • Frustrated by the lack of explanations from the system.
  • Determined to connect via the AirMedia method.

  • Looks at all of the different elements of the interface for affordances.
  • Rereads the steps outlined by the application window.
  • Searches for the support page in order to understand what to do.

  • “This must be somewhere on the screen.”
  • “The words on the screen do not match those on the tablet.”
  • “I guess all of the elements are somewhere on the tablet.”

Bookworm Barry

Mental Model

The two methods of projecting something are as follows: one using cords and the other not. Plugging a cable into a machine is intuitive; however, connecting machines wirelessly involves many complex steps.

Empathy Map

  • AirMedia is a useless method since the cords are so much easier.
  • Why are there so many dongles? What do they all do?
  • There has to be a manual somewhere that explains this better.

  • Confused about the complexity of the system.
  • Frustrated by all the steps needed to connect via AirMedia.
  • Confident in their ability to project something one way or another.

  • Looks around the tablet to see where one of the cords goes.
  • Takes a while to find the AirPlay icon in the instructions.
  • Asks the interviewers for hints on what next step to take.

  • “I guess this cord goes into this port? But what about these ones?”
  • “Where is the password on here? All I see is ‘Code’.”
  • “AirMedia is so extra. The Laptop method gets the job done better.”

Storyboard

Bookwarm Barry has very little experience using a projector, and thus, is a little worried about their important work presentation. Barry in this storyboard experiences a range of emotions in response to the AirMedia method: confusion, hesitation, frustration, and anxiety. They address the explicitly difficult parts of the interface, including, the lack of explanations and poor word mappings between the computer and the tablet. Barry’s apparent inexperience with technology comes out in the form of emotional responses as well as frustration in actions, resembling their respective persona created beforehand.
